01

Listening

Your experience comes before a treatment name.

Being listened to means you do not have to arrive with the right terminology or defend why something matters to you. We begin with what you notice, how it affects you and what kind of change would still feel like your own.

How this feels in practice

You can explain it your way

Sensitive concerns are heard without rushing, correcting or minimising them.

Questions are welcome

Uncertainty is useful information, not an obstacle to move past.

02

Suitability

The right option is the one that fits you now.

Suitability protects you from having a treatment simply because it is popular, available or worked for somebody else. A responsible recommendation considers your health context, skin or tissue condition, timing, recovery and expectations together.

Assess the whole picture

History, sensitivities, current condition and previous procedures can change what is appropriate.

Choose the right moment

Care may be prepared, postponed or simplified when recovery does not fit your circumstances.

Take another route

Sometimes the useful answer is observation, a different approach or referral to another professional.

What this value permits

“Not yet” and “not this treatment” are valid clinical answers.

03

Clarity

Understanding is part of consent.

Clear care turns technical information into something you can actually use. You should know why an option is being considered, what it may improve, what it cannot guarantee and what it asks of you.

  1. Before deciding

    Purpose and alternatives

    We discuss the goal, other reasonable routes, important limits and likely sequence.

  2. Before treatment

    Preparation and recovery

    You know about expected temporary reactions and practical restrictions before committing.

  3. After treatment

    Care and contact

    You receive relevant aftercare and understand which changes are expected or should be reported.

Clarity leaves room for a considered yes, a confident no or more time to think.

— Franciele Sofiati
04

Restraint

Enough is a clinical decision too.

More areas, more sessions or more intensity do not automatically create better care. Restraint means every step needs a reason—and preserving what already feels right matters as much as changing what concerns you.

01

Start with the smallest useful plan

A focused intervention may answer the concern without unnecessary additions.

02

Allow time to review

Healing and response can provide better direction than planning everything at once.

03

Protect expression

Facial planning considers movement and recognisable features, not a standardised ideal.

05

Individuality

Your life belongs inside the plan.

Individual care is more than changing settings on a device. It means considering your features, biology, routine, comfort with recovery and personal definition of a natural result.

A plan shaped around you

Your starting point
Assessment is based on what is present now, not another person’s photograph.
Your pace
Intervals can reflect work, family, social plans and comfort with downtime.
Your response
What actually happens guides whether care continues, changes or stops.
06

Safety

Safety is a sequence, not a single precaution.

It begins before a procedure and continues after you leave. It also includes the judgment to adjust, postpone or advise against elective treatment when that is the safer answer.

  1. 01

    Screen

    Review relevant health information, medication, sensitivities and current condition.

  2. 02

    Prepare

    Give procedure-specific guidance and make sure the area is ready.

  3. 03

    Treat

    Use a selected technique for a defined purpose within professional scope.

  4. 04

    Support

    Explain recovery, warning signs and the appropriate contact route.

07

Honesty

Real expectations protect confidence.

Honesty means talking about uncertainty without making care feel discouraging. Results, comfort, recovery and durability differ between people; examples can inform you, but they cannot promise your response.

What you may hear

“Improvement may be gradual.”

Some responses become visible through healing, remodelling or a planned series.

What you may hear

“The change may be modest.”

A realistic limit is more useful than an exaggerated promise.

What you may hear

“We should change the plan.”

Your actual response can support continuing, simplifying, pausing or stopping.

An honest answer is not less caring. It is how care remains worthy of your trust.

08

Respect

You remain the person with the final say.

Respect keeps your dignity, privacy and boundaries inside every clinical decision. Consultation creates understanding; it never creates an obligation to proceed.

You deserve care that protects your sense of self as carefully as it considers a result.

— Franciele Sofiati

What respect looks like

  • Sensitive concerns are discussed discreetly.
  • You can ask for time without being pressured.
  • You can decline an option or change your mind.
  • Photography and wider image use require appropriate permission.
  • Questions are answered without making you feel difficult.
