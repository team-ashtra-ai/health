**WHY IT MATTERS**

### Care That Respects You

I want aesthetic care to leave you feeling more informed and more comfortable in your own decisions. That means making room for what concerns you without turning every natural feature into something that needs treatment.

You deserve an honest conversation about what may help, what may not and what can reasonably be expected.

#### What you can expect

- Time to explain the concern in your own words
- A recommendation with a clear reason
- Respect for your features, preferences and boundaries
- Preparation and aftercare that make sense for the procedure

#### What I will not do

- Create urgency around an elective decision
- Recommend more simply because more is available
- Promise a result that cannot be guaranteed
- Proceed when timing, health or skin condition makes waiting safer

> You deserve care that helps you feel more confident, while still feeling completely like yourself.

**THE CHANGE**

### Listening Changes the Plan

#### Your concern

I first want to understand what you notice, how it affects you and why you are seeking guidance now.

#### Your context

Health history, medication, previous procedures, routine and upcoming commitments can all change what is sensible.

#### Your priorities

The degree of change you want and the recovery you can manage belong in the decision.

#### Your questions

Uncertainty is welcome. Questions help make consent more informed and the plan more useful.

Listening is not a courtesy added to treatment. It is clinical information that can change the recommendation.

**MY PROMISE**

### Recommendations with Reasons

1. Listen Understand the concern, the hoped-for change and what would still feel natural to you.
2. Assess Consider the area, your history, current condition, previous care and possible contraindications.
3. Explain Describe suitable options, limits, preparation, recovery and alternatives in plain language.
4. Decide Give you space to proceed, prepare, wait or decline without being rushed.
5. Review Use your response to decide whether to continue, adjust, simplify or stop.

> Confidence grows when you understand the choice and know that it remains yours.

**SCIENCE WITH HUMANITY**

### Knowledge with Warmth

#### Clinical judgment

Technology, treatment depth and settings need to match the intended target and the person receiving care.

#### Human attention

Comfort, privacy, fears and practical limitations deserve the same careful conversation.

#### Clear limitations

I will explain uncertainty and what a procedure cannot do, not only its possible benefits.

> You should never have to choose between feeling cared for and receiving an honest answer.

**NATURAL BEAUTY**

### Your Features, Respected

My aim is not to standardise faces, skin or bodies. I consider proportion, movement and the features that make you recognisable before discussing change.

#### Expression

Treatment planning should leave room for the way you naturally communicate and move.

#### Proportion

Another person’s photograph cannot define what will look or feel right for you.

#### Restraint

A smaller intervention, a slower plan or no procedure may be the most appropriate answer.

#### Confidence

Care should support how you feel, not create a new list of things to fear or correct.

> Beauty feels most personal when your character is still present in the result.

_Current visual context: Franciele Sofiati providing individual care that respects natural features_

### Support around Treatment

1. Prepare Receive instructions that reflect the specific procedure and your current skin or treatment area.

- Speak about a sensitive concern without embarrassment.

- Say when a result, recovery period or treatment idea does not feel right.

- Change your mind before giving procedure-specific consent.

A calm clinic experience is not about silence. It is about having enough space to speak, understand and make a considered choice.

> Your questions are part of your care, and you deserve answers you can genuinely understand.

**THE FUTURE I BELIEVE IN**

### Responsibility in Practice

#### Sometimes treatment waits

Active irritation, recent sun exposure, health changes or an unsuitable recovery window may make postponement appropriate.

#### Sometimes less is enough

A simpler routine or a smaller intervention can be more useful than adding procedures.

#### Sometimes another opinion matters

Medical symptoms, uncertain diagnoses or concerns outside aesthetic scope should be assessed by the appropriate professional.

#### Sometimes the plan changes

Progress, tolerance and priorities may lead Franciele to alter intervals, settings or the treatment route.

New technology is valuable when it improves judgment and care, not when it replaces them.

> The highest standard is not doing everything possible; it is choosing what is responsible for you.

**Listening**

### Your experience comes before a treatment name.

#### How this feels in practice

##### Questions are welcome

_Current visual context: A calm, private setting for a personal consultation_

**Suitability**

### The right option is the one that fits you now.

Suitability protects you from having a treatment simply because it is popular, available or worked for somebody else. A responsible recommendation considers your health context, skin or tissue condition, timing, recovery and expectations together.

#### Assess the whole picture

History, sensitivities, current condition and previous procedures can change what is appropriate.

#### Choose the right moment

Care may be prepared, postponed or simplified when recovery does not fit your circumstances.

#### Take another route

Sometimes the useful answer is observation, a different approach or referral to another professional.

What this value permits

“Not yet” and “not this treatment” are valid clinical answers.

**Clarity**

### Understanding is part of consent.

Clear care turns technical information into something you can actually use. You should know why an option is being considered, what it may improve, what it cannot guarantee and what it asks of you.

1. Before deciding Purpose and alternatives We discuss the goal, other reasonable routes, important limits and likely sequence.
2. Before treatment Preparation and recovery You know about expected temporary reactions and practical restrictions before committing.
3. After treatment Care and contact You receive relevant aftercare and understand which changes are expected or should be reported.

> Clarity leaves room for a considered yes, a confident no or more time to think. — Franciele Sofiati

**Restraint**

### Enough is a clinical decision too.

More areas, more sessions or more intensity do not automatically create better care. Restraint means every step needs a reason—and preserving what already feels right matters as much as changing what concerns you.

02

#### Allow time to review

Healing and response can provide better direction than planning everything at once.

03

#### Protect expression

#### A plan shaped around you

**Safety**

### Safety is a sequence, not a single precaution.

It begins before a procedure and continues after you leave. It also includes the judgment to adjust, postpone or advise against elective treatment when that is the safer answer.

**MY CARE PHILOSOPHY**

### A Plan That Fits

You do not need to arrive knowing the name of a treatment. You may come with a change you have noticed, a previous experience you want to discuss or a result you would like to understand.

I begin with that context rather than immediately directing you towards a procedure.

- Context before procedure names;
- Assessment before recommendation;
- Natural-looking outcomes without standardising every person;
- Honest explanations without guaranteed results;
- Preparation and aftercare as part of the treatment;
- Space to ask, reconsider or wait.

> Your recovery deserves the same attention as the treatment itself.

_Current visual context: Franciele Sofiati discussing preparation and recovery with a patient_

**HOW I BUILD A PLAN**

### How Preparation Is Decided

1. Listen Understand what brought you here and what matters to you.
2. Review Discuss history, routine, sensitivities, health context and timing.
3. Assess Evaluate the skin, scalp, face, body area or vascular concern.
4. Explain Clarify options, limits, sensations, risks and recovery.
5. Decide Treat, prepare, stage, refer, observe or wait.

#### Evaluation first. Then, the appropriate next step.

A consultation may lead to a procedure, but its first purpose is to create clarity.

**BEFORE TREATMENT**

### What Franciele Needs to Know

#### Health and medication

Relevant conditions, allergies, medication, supplements, pregnancy, breastfeeding and recent illness may affect planning.

#### Previous treatments

Lasers, peels, injectables, surgery, threads, tattoos and reactions can remain relevant.

#### Current products

Retinoids, acids, acne products, depigmenting formulas and home devices may affect tolerance.

#### Sun and heat exposure

Recent tanning, sunburn or planned outdoor activity can change treatment timing.

#### Your calendar

Travel, work, exercise, celebrations and photographs help determine an appropriate recovery window.

#### Your expectations

We discuss what may improve, what may require several stages and what cannot be promised.

> Preparing well is a quiet way of caring for the result and for yourself.

**DIFFERENT TREATMENTS, DIFFERENT RECOVERY**

### Recovery Is Treatment-Specific

#### Cleansing and gentle renewal

Deep cleansing, diamond peel, crystal peel and ultrasonic peel may require a simple, calming routine and temporary pause from irritating products.

#### Chemical renewal

Retinoic, Jessner, TCA and depigmentation protocols vary in depth. Flaking, redness and product restrictions depend on the exact peel.

#### Laser and resurfacing

Hair reduction, pigment treatment and ablative CO₂ resurfacing have very different recovery needs.

#### Firmness and energy-based care

Focused ultrasound, radiofrequency and plasma technologies differ in target, depth, sensation and aftercare.

#### Injectables and microinfusion

Botulinum toxin, MMP and scalp injections require treatment-specific guidance on pressure, products and activity.

#### Superficial microvessels

PEIM aftercare depends on the vessels treated, sclerosant used and individual vascular assessment.

**AFTERCARE FOUNDATIONS**

### The Foundations of Aftercare

The instructions I provide after your appointment take priority over general website information.

- Use only the cleansers, moisturisers and repair products recommended for the procedure;
- Avoid repeated touching, rubbing, scratching, squeezing or pressing;
- Allow flakes, peeling or small crusts to release naturally;
- Follow the sun-protection plan discussed for your treatment;
- Ask before restarting retinoids, acids, scrubs or depigmenting products;
- Keep anything that contacts the treated area clean.

> Gentle, consistent care can be more valuable than doing too much.

_Current visual context: Franciele Sofiati providing treatment with aftercare in mind_

**DOS AND DON’TS**

### Helpful Choices at Home

#### Do

- Follow your individual instructions
- Keep the routine simple and consistent
- Respect the recovery window
- Send clear photographs when requested
- Ask before combining treatments
- Contact me when uncertain

#### Avoid

- Picking, scratching or peeling
- Copying another patient’s routine
- Adding strong active products early
- Unprotected sun exposure
- Judging every result immediately
- Treating aftercare as optional

> You deserve to feel supported, even after you leave the treatment room.

**DAILY LIFE DURING RECOVERY**

### Daily Life During Recovery

#### Exercise

Intense activity may need to be postponed after selected injectables, vascular care or deeper resurfacing.

#### Heat

Hot showers, saunas, steam and prolonged heat may be unsuitable during early recovery.

#### Swimming

Pools, hot tubs, lakes and the sea may need to wait while the barrier is disrupted.

#### Makeup

It may be allowed quickly after some procedures and restricted after others.

#### Shaving and waxing

Hair-removal methods may need to be adjusted around laser, peels or resurfacing.

#### Massage and pressure

Do not massage injectable or energy-treated areas unless specifically instructed.

#### Individual guidance only

I do not impose every possible restriction after every treatment. I explain what applies to the procedure you actually received.

**CONTACT AND SAFETY**

### When to Get in Touch

Temporary redness, warmth, tenderness, swelling, bruising, dryness or peeling may occur after some procedures. The expected response depends on the treatment and should be explained before you leave.

- Contact me if pain is severe or increasing;
- Contact me if redness, swelling or heat continues to spread;
- Contact me for blistering, discharge, an unexpected wound or marked colour change;
- Contact me if altered sensation persists or intensifies;
- Contact me before resuming a product or activity when you are unsure.

#### Seek urgent medical care

Do not wait for a routine clinic reply for difficulty breathing, speaking or swallowing; rapid facial or throat swelling; fainting; severe generalised weakness; significant visual disturbance; chest pain; sudden shortness of breath; coughing blood; or another serious, rapidly developing reaction.

Franciele Sofiati · Biomedical Aesthetics

**BENEATH THE SURFACE**

### Look beyond the Surface

#### Epidermis

The outer region supports barrier function, pigment production and continual surface renewal.

#### Dermis

Contains collagen, elastin, vessels, nerve endings, follicles and glands contributing to strength and support.

#### Supporting tissue

Contributes to cushioning, volume, contour and the relationship between skin and deeper structures.

#### Why depth matters

A cleanser, peel, CO₂ laser, radiofrequency and focused ultrasound do not act in the same tissue plane.

#### Concern, target and depth

These three elements should agree before a professional treatment is chosen.

**THE SKIN BARRIER**

### Supporting the Skin Barrier

When skin asks for less, I listen.

#### Signs it may be overwhelmed

- Tightness after cleansing
- Persistent dryness or roughness
- Burning or stinging
- Increased redness
- Flaking
- Sudden reaction to familiar products

#### Common sources of pressure

- Too many active ingredients
- Repeated exfoliation
- Harsh cleansing
- Unprotected sun or heat
- Poorly timed procedures
- Constantly changing routines

#### Sometimes the first treatment is repair

Fewer products, gentler cleansing, barrier-supportive moisturising, sun protection and time can create a better foundation.

> Sometimes the kindest thing you can give your skin is less.

**A USEFUL ROUTINE**

### A Routine with a Purpose

#### Morning

- Cleanse according to need
- Use a targeted product when appropriate
- Moisturise for comfort and support
- Apply broad-spectrum sun protection

#### Evening

- Remove makeup and sunscreen
- Cleanse without scrubbing
- Use actives at a planned frequency
- Support the barrier as needed

#### Before adding another product, ask:

- What is it intended to do?
- Am I already using something for the same purpose?
- Could it irritate another active?
- How often should I begin?
- What result would be realistic?
- Will I use it consistently enough to evaluate it?

> A routine should support your confidence, not make care feel impossible.

**YOUR SKIN CONCERN ATLAS**

### Common Skin Concerns

#### Acne and congestion

Blackheads, whiteheads, inflammatory lesions, oil and post-acne marks do not all need the same approach.

#### Pigmentation

Dark marks may have different causes, depths and triggers.

#### Sensitivity and redness

Burning, stinging, flushing or persistent redness may reflect irritation or another condition.

#### Texture and pores

Pores are normal structures whose appearance can be influenced by oil, congestion, elasticity and light.

#### Marks and scars

Flat colour changes and structural scars are different concerns.

#### Lines and photoageing

Ageing reflects time, genetics, movement, hormones and environmental exposure.

**SKIN MYTHS**

### Skin Advice, Reconsidered

#### “Oily skin does not need moisturiser.”

Oil and water balance are not the same. Oily or acne-prone skin can still become irritated and uncomfortable.

#### “Blackheads are dirt.”

Blackheads are open comedones. Their dark appearance comes from follicular contents being exposed to air—not poor hygiene.

#### “Scrubbing clears acne faster.”

Aggressive scrubbing can increase irritation, colour change and discomfort.

#### “Pores can be permanently closed.”

Pores are normal anatomical openings. Their appearance may be refined, not removed.

#### “Tingling proves a product is working.”

Tingling can also signal irritation. Effective care does not need to burn.

#### “More peeling means a better treatment.”

Visible peeling is not a reliable measure of quality and can simply indicate more inflammation.

> Comfort is valuable information, not something you need to push through.

**PIGMENT, SUN AND MEMORY**

### Pigmentation Has a History

Pigment can appear or deepen after sunlight, acne, irritation, injury, hormonal change, friction, medication or poorly timed procedures.

#### Pattern

Is it isolated, scattered, symmetrical or widespread?

#### Trigger

Did it follow acne, pregnancy, irritation, treatment or sun exposure?

#### Behaviour

Is it fading, stable, deepening or returning?

#### Skin response

Does the skin inflame or mark easily?

#### Maintenance

Can the necessary routine and protection be followed?

#### Medical review

A new, changing, bleeding or unexplained lesion should not be treated as a routine mark.

#### ☼ Sun protection is a system

Broad-spectrum sunscreen, shade, clothing, sensible exposure and reapplication work together. Tinted protection may be discussed for selected pigmentary concerns.

> Consistency can bring more confidence than chasing every new product.

**ACNE WITHOUT PUNISHMENT**

### A Calmer Approach to Acne

Acne is a complex inflammatory condition involving the follicle, oil production, cell build-up and microbial activity. It is not evidence that the skin is dirty.

#### Acne-prone skin often needs

- Gentle consistent cleansing
- Gradual introduction of actives
- Moisture support
- Sun protection
- Time to evaluate response
- Medical assessment when needed

#### What often makes it harder

- Picking or squeezing
- Changing routines repeatedly
- Several strong ingredients at once
- Scrubbing inflamed skin
- Trying to dry every area
- Treating marks while inflammation continues

> Your skin deserves time, protection and a plan it can tolerate.

**WHAT LASER IS**

### Light with a Defined Purpose

#### Melanin

Pigment in hair and skin. It may be targeted in hair-reduction and selected pigmentary treatments.

#### Haemoglobin

A component of blood targeted by selected technologies for superficial vessels or redness.

#### Water

A principal target in ablative CO₂ resurfacing, allowing controlled microscopic treatment.

#### Tattoo pigment

Selected wavelengths and pulse characteristics can fragment particular ink particles.

#### The essential principle

Target first. Technology second. Settings third. The machine name alone does not establish suitability.

_Current visual context: Franciele Sofiati selecting laser technology for a defined clinical target_

**WHAT LASER IS NOT**

### What Laser Cannot Promise

#### Laser is

- A broad group of different devices
- Selected for a defined target
- Dependent on wavelength and settings
- Planned around skin and recovery

#### Laser is not

- One procedure for every concern
- The same as IPL
- Automatically better at higher energy
- A guarantee of perfect or identical results

Laser cannot responsibly promise poreless skin, complete scar removal, permanent removal of every hair or the same result for every person.

> Technology should support your confidence, never replace careful judgment.

**WHEN LASER MAY BE USED**

### Different Targets, Different Lasers

#### Hair reduction

Progressive long-term reduction in the number, density or thickness of suitable hairs.

#### Pigmentation

Selected pigmented lesions or uneven colour after the cause and pattern are considered.

#### Vessels and redness

Selected superficial vessels according to colour, depth, size and location.

#### Texture, wrinkles and scars

Controlled thermal or ablative treatment that may support remodelling.

#### Tattoos and micropigmentation

Fragmentation of selected pigments over a planned series.

#### Resurfacing

A more intensive renewal pathway with meaningful preparation and recovery.

> Your skin deserves the option that fits it, not simply the strongest one.

**TECHNOLOGIES I USE**

### Technologies Used in Practice

#### Harmony Laser Platform

Harmony is a multi-technology platform. Its applications depend on the exact handpiece, wavelength and settings selected. According to the available configuration, it may be considered for selected pigmentary, vascular, texture, hair-reduction, scar or tattoo-related concerns.

#### LightSheer Duet

LightSheer Duet is a diode-laser system used for progressive long-term hair reduction. Treatment is adapted according to skin tone, hair colour and thickness, treatment area, recent sun exposure and previous response.

#### AcuPulse CO₂

AcuPulse is an ablative carbon-dioxide laser used for selected resurfacing treatments. Because it intentionally affects the skin barrier, it requires more preparation and recovery than a superficial facial procedure.

**LASER HAIR REDUCTION**

### Understanding Hair Reduction

Hair grows in phases. Because only part of the hair is in a responsive stage at each appointment, treatment is usually planned as a series.

#### Hair colour

Darker hair generally offers a clearer pigment target. Very light, grey, white and some red hair may respond poorly.

#### Hair thickness

Coarser and finer hair may respond differently.

#### Skin tone

Melanin is also present in the skin, so device and settings must respect surrounding tissue.

#### Recent tanning

Tanning changes energy absorption and may affect treatment timing.

#### Treatment area

Facial and body hair do not always follow the same rhythm.

#### Hormonal influences

Some hair patterns may continue to develop and may require maintenance or investigation.

#### What it may offer

- Fewer suitable hairs
- Slower regrowth
- Finer remaining hair
- Longer intervals between regrowth

#### What it cannot guarantee

- Removal of every hair
- The same number of sessions for everyone
- No future hormonal hair growth
- A strong response from hair without enough pigment

> A gradual result can still be a meaningful and beautiful change.

_Current visual context: Franciele Sofiati carrying out laser hair reduction treatment_

**CO₂ RESURFACING**

### Planning CO₂ Resurfacing

Fractional does not mean superficial.

Fractional CO₂ laser treats microscopic areas while leaving surrounding skin untreated. This pattern supports healing, but the procedure still intentionally disrupts the barrier.

- Selected acne scars and other carefully assessed scars;
- Fine lines and wrinkles;
- Uneven texture;
- Selected signs of sun-related skin change.

Depth and density affect both the possible treatment response and the recovery burden. More intensity can mean more redness, swelling, peeling, sensitivity and risk.

> Good preparation gives your skin the respect it deserves.

**WHEN TO WAIT**

### When Treatment Should Wait

#### Recent tanning or sunburn

Additional pigment or inflammation can change risk.

#### Active irritation

Sensitive or injured skin may need time and supportive care.

#### Infection

Active bacterial, viral or fungal concerns should be assessed first.

#### An unexplained lesion

A changing, bleeding or irregular mark should not simply be treated as pigmentation.

#### Medication or health considerations

Some conditions, healing patterns or medicines may affect treatment choice.

#### Limited recovery time

An intensive procedure should not be forced into an unsuitable calendar.

> Waiting can be a positive choice when it protects your skin.

**BEFORE, DURING AND AFTER**

### From Preparation to Recovery

1. Before Review concern, history, medication, products, previous procedures and sun exposure.
2. Explain Clarify target, technology, sensation, expected reactions, limits and recovery.
3. Protect Prepare the area and use appropriate eye protection.
4. Treat Select the handpiece and settings for the plan while monitoring comfort and response.
5. After Assess the area and provide procedure-specific aftercare.

#### Possible temporary responses

Depending on the technology: redness, warmth, tenderness, swelling, darkening of treated pigment, dryness, crusting or peeling.

_Current visual context: A clinical result considered within the full treatment context_

**RESULT FACTORS**

### Your Starting Point Matters

The treatment name is only one part of the result. Baseline condition, biology, technique, timing and aftercare all influence what can change.

#### Starting point

Tone, texture, scars, laxity, hair pattern or movement define the baseline.

#### Treatment details

Device, product, settings, depth, technique and session number matter.

#### Healing time

Some results appear quickly; others need recovery or tissue remodeling.

#### Aftercare

Sun exposure, routine, habits and follow-up can support or limit progress.

#### Expectation note

Similar treatments do not create identical results. Your own assessment is the reference point.

**TIMING**

### Results Have Their Own Timing

#### Immediate effects

Some redness, swelling or temporary surface change may be visible first.

#### Early recovery

Peeling, bruising or treated pigment may temporarily change appearance.

#### Developing effect

Botulinum toxin and tissue-remodelling treatments need time before review.

#### Series-based care

Hair reduction, pigmentation and scar plans may involve several sessions.

#### Long-term response

Ageing, hormones, sun exposure and recurrence can influence maintenance.

#### Review point

Results should be assessed at an appropriate interval—not simply as soon as possible.

**PHOTOGRAPHY INTEGRITY**

### Fair Before-and-After Comparisons

#### A useful comparison keeps

- Similar lighting
- Similar angle and distance
- Similar expression and posture
- No beauty filters
- A clear treatment interval
- The same area visible

#### A misleading comparison may use

- Different shadows or exposure
- Different facial expression
- Makeup in only one image
- Retouching or smoothing
- Immediate swelling as a final result
- Unstated treatment combinations

#### Context belongs beside the image

When possible, result content should identify the treatment, number of sessions, timing and relevant limitations without revealing private patient information.

**NATURAL-LOOKING RESULTS**

### Improvement That Still Looks Like You

Natural-looking care is not one fixed aesthetic. It means the degree of change respects the person’s anatomy, expression, preferences and sense of self.

#### Expression

Movement is considered rather than automatically treated as a problem.

#### Proportion

The face or body is assessed as a whole rather than as isolated flaws.

#### Restraint

More treatment is not assumed to create a better outcome.

#### Preference

A subtle change and a more visible change are not the same request.

#### Identity

The objective is not to copy another person’s appearance.

#### Time

Gradual change may feel more appropriate than an immediate transformation.

> A photograph is only an example; your own progress deserves its own measure.

_Current visual context: Franciele Sofiati preserving individuality during treatment planning_

**IMPROVEMENT IS NOT ERASURE**

### Honest Words about Change

#### Care may help

- Soften selected lines
- Reduce suitable hair
- Improve selected pigment
- Refine texture
- Soften scar features
- Support mild tightening

#### Care should not promise

- No movement or pores
- Removal of every hair forever
- Permanent control of every pigment condition
- Perfectly smooth skin
- Complete scar disappearance
- A surgical result from a non-surgical treatment

> Natural confidence grows from improvement that still feels like you.

**MAINTENANCE AND RECURRENCE**

### Sessions and Maintenance

Aesthetic care does not stop time or remove every trigger. Results may evolve as the body, skin and lifestyle continue to change.

- Botulinum toxin is temporary;
- Hair may need maintenance, especially when hormonal influences continue;
- Melasma and some pigmentation can recur;
- Skin ageing and sun exposure continue;
- Home care can support—but not freeze—a result;
- Future treatment should be based on review rather than an automatic schedule.

**REVIEWING RESULTS**

### When You Have Concerns

First, compare the response with the expected timeline and treatment information. Some effects settle gradually; others need prompt assessment.

#### Too early to judge

The treatment may still be healing or developing.

#### Unexpected reaction

Pain, spreading redness, blistering or another unusual change needs contact.

#### Expectation mismatch

The original goal and treatment limits may need to be discussed again.

#### Asymmetry or uneven response

Anatomy, swelling and treatment response should be evaluated at the correct time.

#### Further treatment

Another session should not be assumed before the first response is reviewed.

#### No further treatment

Sometimes the most appropriate decision is to stop, wait or choose another route.

**A note from me**

### You do not need to know which treatment you need

Many people arrive with the name of a procedure they have seen online. Others simply know that something has changed in their skin, hair or body, but they are not sure what to ask for. Both are completely normal places to begin.

During your consultation, I want to understand you before I recommend anything. I will ask what you have noticed, what you have already tried, how the concern affects you and what kind of result would still feel natural and comfortable.

Sometimes I may recommend a treatment. Sometimes the best next step is preparation, a simpler option, more time, another assessment or no procedure at all. My role is to give you clear and honest guidance—not to persuade you to do more.

_Current visual context: Franciele Sofiati listening carefully during an aesthetic consultation in Londrina_

**How your consultation works**

### A calm appointment with time to talk, assess and decide

You can explain everything in your own words. I will guide the conversation and ask the questions that help me understand the full picture.

1. First, I listen You tell me what you have noticed, what bothers you most and what made you decide to seek professional guidance now.
2. Then, we look at the context We talk about your routine, previous procedures, sensitivities, sun exposure, important dates and anything else that may affect your plan.
3. I assess the area carefully During an in-person consultation, I examine the area and consider the individual characteristics that may influence treatment suitability.
4. I explain the possibilities I talk you through the options that may be appropriate, including their purpose, limits, preparation, comfort, recovery and follow-up.
5. You decide without pressure You can proceed, prepare first, wait, consider another option or decide not to have a procedure. The final decision remains yours.

**How I plan your care**

### A recommendation should fit your life—not only the treatment area

#### What matters to you

I want to know what you would genuinely like to improve, how much change you are comfortable with and what would make the result feel worthwhile to you.

#### What is appropriate

I consider your history, skin or hair characteristics, previous procedures, sensitivities, routine, timing and the recovery you can realistically manage.

#### What is realistic

I will be honest about expected improvement, limitations, session planning, maintenance and the fact that every person responds differently.

> You deserve a recommendation that respects your features, your confidence and your right to choose.

**Before we meet**

### You do not need to prepare perfectly

A few simple details can help me understand your concern more quickly. Bring what you know, and we can work through the rest together.

#### Helpful things to remember

- Products you currently use in the area
- Previous procedures and approximate dates
- Medication and supplements that may be relevant; do not stop prescribed treatment without medical guidance
- Known allergies, sensitivities or unusual reactions
- Recent tanning, strong sun exposure or irritation
- Photographs showing how the concern has changed, when useful

#### Plans that may affect timing

- Travel or periods of prolonged sun exposure
- Weddings, celebrations, photographs or important work events
- Your available recovery time
- Work, exercise or care responsibilities
- Your preferred treatment period

> Looking after yourself also means giving your body the time, honesty and patience it needs.

**What happens afterwards**

### My aim is for you to leave with clarity

By the end of the consultation, you should understand what I recommend, why I recommend it and what the next step would involve.

#### Your next step may include

- A suitable treatment or treatment sequence
- Preparation before beginning
- Expected sessions, spacing and review
- Recovery and aftercare guidance
- A gentler alternative or a decision to wait
- Referral for another professional assessment when appropriate

#### You will not be rushed

You are welcome to ask questions and take time to think. Booking a consultation does not commit you to a procedure, and no responsible consultation can promise an exact result.

Photographs and messages may help with the first contact, but they do not replace an appropriate assessment when one is needed.

# Content and visual-direction analysis

## Executive diagnosis

The site has a clear and unusually responsible point of view: consultation before intervention, context before comparison, restraint before escalation, and aftercare as part of treatment. That is the brand. The strongest mockups should make this judgment-led approach visible rather than defaulting to generic luxury-clinic imagery.

The current copy is comprehensive, reassuring and ethically careful. Its main design challenge is volume and recurrence. Twenty-one routes repeatedly use ten-chapter structures, card collections, paired contrasts and cautionary statements. If every idea receives the same card treatment, equally weighted sections make the experience feel longer and flatter than the underlying content deserves. The redesign opportunity is not “more decoration”; it is sharper hierarchy, more evidence-specific imagery and more distinct page identities.

## What the content says the brand actually is

1. **A guide, not a seller.** The dominant verbs are understand, assess, explain, prepare, review and adapt. Imagery should show listening, observation and planning as often as procedures.
2. **Clinically careful but not hospital-cold.** The copy combines safety limits with warm human language. The visual world should combine precise framing, honest light and tactile natural materials.
3. **Anti-hype and anti-erasure.** Results are discussed as individual, time-dependent and contextual. Avoid glossy “perfect skin,” dramatic transformation tropes and visual language that suggests guaranteed outcomes.
4. **Continuity is a service.** Preparation, recovery, contact and review recur throughout the site. Timelines, care cards, annotated sequences and follow-up moments can make continuity tangible.
5. **Franciele’s judgment is the differentiator.** Technology and treatment names are tools. The professional’s evaluation is the connecting story. Portraits should therefore feel attentive and working, not merely posed.

## Content strengths to preserve

- Headings frequently work as memorable principles: “Care without punishment,” “Improvement is not erasure,” and “The image is only one layer.” These are strong editorial anchors.
- The copy answers anxious, real-world questions about timing, discomfort, suitability, recovery and uncertainty.
- Safety language is specific enough to be useful without turning the whole brand alarmist.
- The treatment content consistently explains limits, which creates credibility.
- Cross-links support exploratory visitors who know their concern but not the name of a procedure.
- Forms collect useful context and explain why information is requested.

## Content risks the mockups should solve

### 1. Repetition can make important distinctions disappear

Words such as context, clarity, individual, responsible, pressure, promise and understanding recur across many pages. They belong to the brand, but when every chapter uses the same visual weight, readers may remember the tone while missing the practical difference between pages.

**Mockup response:** give each major page family one unmistakable visual grammar. Skin can feel observational and editorial; Laser can feel precise and light-led; Results can resemble an evidence ledger; Care can use time and recovery sequencing; About can feel biographical and personal.

### 2. Ten-section pages produce uniform narrative pacing

The pages contain good material, but ten similarly sized chapters can feel like ten equal stops. Visitors need a visible distinction between the core answer, supporting explanation, evidence, safety limits and next action.

**Mockup response:** design three pacing levels: one dominant opening proposition, two or three expanded teaching chapters, and compact supporting chapters. Use image-led pauses or concise diagrams between dense text groups.

### 3. The visual evidence is less specific than the written evidence

The copy is precise about assessment, technologies, treatment intervals and photography integrity, while much of the current imagery is portrait or interior photography. That creates a gap between what the site says and what it proves visually.

**Mockup response:** add procedure-specific but restrained evidence: consultation notes, device handpieces, protective preparation, standardized photography setup, treatment-room details and aftercare materials. Never show invasive detail merely for drama.

### 4. Negative constructions dominate some chapters

“Not,” “without,” “cannot,” “should not” and “do not” are valuable for setting safe limits, but too many consecutive warnings can make the experience feel defensive.

**Mockup response:** pair boundaries with a positive visual route. A caution card can sit beside “what we do instead,” a preparation checklist, a review path or a calm human moment.

### 5. Conversion moments are repeated but not differentiated

“Request a consultation,” “Contact the clinic” and similar actions appear often. The actions are appropriate, but visitors may not understand when a simple message is enough and when a consultation is the better next step.

**Mockup response:** use a consistent two-route decision component: **I have a practical question** versus **I want an individual assessment**. Show response expectations, information needed and urgency limits directly in the component.

### 6. Trust is stated more often than demonstrated

Credentials, responsible language and patient words are present, but they can be integrated into richer proof.

**Mockup response:** create an evidence strip with credentials, years/areas of practice if supportable, clinic location, photography standards, aftercare access and sourced patient feedback. Only include verifiable facts; do not invent numbers.

## A coherent photography shot list

One disciplined shoot can supply most of the authentic assets needed for the mockups without repeating the same portrait everywhere:

1. **Consultation conversation:** wide, medium and detail crops; Franciele listening, explaining and taking notes; patient identity optional or kept outside frame.
2. **Working portraits:** three distinct moods—welcoming, analytical and reflective—with natural skin texture and consistent colour treatment.
3. **Clinic architecture:** reception, treatment room, consultation surface, doorway and quiet material details; level architectural lines and both horizontal/vertical crops.
4. **Assessment details:** non-diagnostic observation, note-taking, calendar/timing discussion and preparation explanation.
5. **Technology families:** accurate handpieces, protective preparation and device controls photographed consistently; no simulated treatment effect.
6. **Standardized result photography:** neutral background, fixed light, camera position, distance marker and metadata card.
7. **Aftercare continuity:** written guidance, sunscreen, clean towel, calendar, phone follow-up and review conversation.
8. **Editorial skin/routine still life:** neutral objects and diverse natural skin detail, with no brand sponsorship implied.

Shoot every essential setup in landscape, portrait and close-detail crops. Reserve clean negative space on both left and right so the same authentic material can support responsive editorial layouts without aggressive cropping.

# Recommended production order

1. Prototype Mockups 01, 03, 06 and 09 first. Together they test brand, catalogue, evidence and conversion.
2. Build the mobile contact sheet before expanding desktop pages; this will reveal where long copy needs progressive disclosure.
3. Commission one coherent authentic photo library from a shot list derived from these mockups: consultation, working portrait, clinic environment, standardized photography setup, device/preparation details and aftercare/follow-up.
4. Reuse layout systems, not identical compositions. Home, Treatments, Results and Consultation should remain visibly different.
5. Test all mockups with the longest real headings and Portuguese expansion even though this deck contains English copy.

# Guardrails for image creation

- Do not fabricate before-and-after results, testimonials, credentials or clinical settings and present them as real.
- Preserve natural skin texture and individual features; avoid beauty-filter aesthetics.
- Obtain specific consent for identifiable patients, treatment moments and result photography.
- Keep equipment accurate and secondary to professional judgment.
- Do not use imagery that implies one technology is suitable for everyone.
- Pair safety copy with clear action, not alarming imagery.
- Maintain consistent lighting, angle, crop and timing metadata for any real comparison.
- Write useful alt text during production and treat decorative textures as decorative.
